Driven to suicide and shaking under the Christmas tree alone... brave women share what it's like to

Publish date: 2024-08-20

WITH family rows, the pressure of buying presents and the dreaded journey home, Christmas can often feel like too much to handle.

But for the 10million Brits plagued by suicidal thoughts, Christmas can prove an agonising time.

Here, three brave women speak out as part of the Sun’s You’re Not Alone campaign, sharing how they have been driven to the brink of suicide - and how they've overcome their darkest days.

'I spent Christmas Day shaking under the tree'

Ailsa Frank is 51 from Camberley, Surrey

She says: "Christmas is the time of year people like me dread the most - in fact, I refused to celebrate it for 13 years. I split up from my husband in 2002 and I lost custody of our daughter after I suffered a mental breakdown.

It had been an awful year, and seeing happy families on TV and remembering our past Christmases as a family was more than I could cope with.

I'd bought a tree to try to make things seem normal. But December 25 took on a new meaning.

I remember lying on the floor underneath it, shaking uncontrollably consumed with pure anxiety.

I wished there was someone there to comfort me, but I wasn't close to my family and didn't have many friends. Watching people buying presents and preparing for Christmas left me feeling so alone.

Seeing adverts on TV, Christmas food in the shops and hearing people discuss their plans were all triggers that could make me burst into tears.

One year I saw a family doing their big Christmas shop in a local supermarket and it was too much - I ran out in tears.

Friends stopped inviting me out - and I wasn't invited to any Christmas or New Year's Eve parties. I spent the whole week alone and depressed.

I’ve lost count of the number of Christmases I spent staring vacantly at the wall. Each year I’d go to bed on Christmas Eve and hope I wouldn’t wake up

Ailsa Frank

The same thing happened the following year. It felt like I was outside the Christmas bubble, trying so hard to get in.

Everything about Christmas reminded me of old memories - now it was the quietest time of year with no traffic on the local streets and for the first time in my life I was aware of the eerie silence.

I've lost count of the number of Christmases I spent staring vacantly at the wall.

Each year I'd go to bed on Christmas Eve and hope I wouldn't wake up. The only thing stopping me from actually trying to kill myself was that I wanted to be there for my daughter.

I only had one friend who used to buy me a present.

One year she said: "This year I am going to give to charity instead of giving presents - is that OK with you?"

I remember freezing because hers was the only gift I was given, my only sense of feeling normal at Christmas.

She didn't realise how important her gift was to me. I didn't say anything and she must have thought I was a bit weird. To her it was a simple thing but to me it stirred all sorts of emotions.

I eventually started going abroad at that time of year to do non-Christmas things as the pain of it was all too great.

YOU'RE NOT ALONE

EVERY 90 minutes in the UK a life is lost - to suicide.

It doesn't discriminate, touching the lives of people in every corner of society - from the homeless and unemployed to builders and doctors, reality stars and footballers.

It's the biggest killer of people under the age of 35, more deadly than cancer and car crashes. And men are three times more likely to take their own life than women.

Yet, it's rarely spoken of, a taboo that threatens to continue its deadly rampage unless we all stop and take notice, now.

That is why The Sun has launched the You're Not Alone campaign, to remind anyone facing a tough time, grappling with mental illness or feeling like there's nowhere left to turn, that there is hope.

The aim is that by sharing practical advice, raising awareness and breaking down the barriers people face when talking about their mental health, we can all do our bit to help save lives.

Let's all vow to ask for help when we need it, and listen out for others. You're Not Alone.

A few years ago, I met someone new and we moved to Shanghai.

I got a job in an orphanage, which was full of hundreds of girls who had been abandoned by their families because of the one child policy -people wanted boys not girls.

This helped me gain some perspective - realising parents around the world are without their children from circumstances out of their control.

I went on to train as a hypnotherapist and now other people overcome their challenges which has given me a new sense of purpose.

Last year, I had my first Christmas with my daughter, now 24, in a log cabin in the Cotswolds, and the magic came flooding back.

Now I can watch Christmas films with joy, and I love picking out the tasty party bits in M&S.

This year, I'm having my whole family over and I can't wait.

But I know for many, the festive season is unbearable."

‘After trying to kill myself, I spiralled into the depths of an eating disorder’

Carly-Jayne Beech is 21 from Peterborough

She says: “I started feeling suicidal when I was about 16 but I'd been self-harming for years before that - things always got a lot worse at Christmas.

When I was feeling down, I’d hurt myself and it felt good for a while – but one day it stopped working and I decided I'd be better off dead.

I felt really low, like I couldn't see a future.

So I took a bunch of pills I'd been hiding in my drawer.

Thinking back, it’s difficult to determine what exactly led me to do it. By all definitions, I had a good life.

Although it was just me and my mum, we had a good relationship. I had a good group of friends, I was doing well at school, I had a strong social life - but I was just unhappy all the time.

Nothing could pull me out of it, I would be fine one second and suicidal the next.

I lived in fear of talking about my feelings and I wouldn’t speak about them to anyone, so when I overdosed, my friends were shocked to say the least.

After swallowing the pills, it was like a switch flicked in my brain. I called for my mum, immediately regretting my decision.

She rushed me to hospital, where nurses made me drink a cup full of charcoal to get them out of my system.

After my attempt, I head was fine – or so I thought. But when Christmas came around later that year I fell into new depths of depression.

While my mates were out enjoying Christmas parties, decorating their houses in fairy lights and buying each other presents, I felt numb.

I couldn't get excited about anything and while everyone around me was happily singing Christmas songs, I wanted to fast forward to January.

I didn't realise it at the time, but in the depths of my misery and depression I stopped eating.

When I was feeling down, I’d hurt myself and it felt good for a while – but one day it stopped working and I decided I’d be better off dead

Carly-Jayne Beech

While everyone else was enjoying stuffing their faces with Camembert and mince pies, I felt sick constantly - and I avoided food altogether.

On Christmas Day, my mum slaved away in the kitchen to make our lunch, but when I sat down to eat the Brussels sprouts and turkey in front of me, I couldn’t lift the fork to my mouth.

I made up an excuse about feeling ill and was riddled with guilt that I hadn't been able to start the Christmas dinner she'd worked so hard on.

Over that Christmas week, things got worse.


Suicide in Britain - the tragic facts

  • 1 in 15 adults in England have attempted suicide
  • 3 out of 4 deaths by suicide are men
  • Suicide is the biggest killer of men under 50
  • Suicide is the biggest killer of women aged 20 to 34
  • Suicide is the leading cause of death for people under 35
  • People diagnosed with a mental health disorder are 5 to 15 times more likely to die by suicide
  • Unemployed people are 2.5 times more likely to die by suicide than employed
  • Low-skilled male labourers, particularly in construction, have a 3 times higher risk of suicide
  • Prisoners are up to 10 times more likely to die by suicide
  • Young people who have been sexually abused are 8 times more likely to attempt suicide
  • Gay and bisexual men are 4 times more likely to attempt suicide than heterosexual men
  • Around 26% of young women have self-harmed
  • 1 in 7 deaths of new mothers is by suicide
  • Homeless people are 5 times more likely to die by suicide

I wasn't trying to lose weight, but even when offered my favourite pigs in blankets I refused. I didn't think I deserved them.

I’ve always been slim – but in the New Year I got on the scales and saw I’d lost 10lbs - and I suddenly felt good about myself.

I became obsessed with being under 7st - I felt more confident and finally in control.

I started weighing out all my meals to make sure I knew exactly how many calories I was eating and my weight plummeted.

Friends started joking that they didn’t need to buy food because they’d just eat what I left - they didn’t realise I had a problem of course, I don’t think anyone realised how serious it was.

It turned into a bit of a joke, with family members joking that my baby niece could eat more of her dinner than I could.

After that, my mum started pointing out that I wasn’t eating much of my dinner at all.

Seeing how worried about me she was, I made a promise to try and start eating again and she threw away the weighing scales.

It was a slow process, but over the next few months I managed to start upping my calorie intake.

I started dating someone and it gave me the confidence that starving myself had been doing before.

While I still avoid finishing a full meal, I am back to a healthy weight - but Christmas is never a good time for me.

I still can't finish everything on my plate, but at least this year I'll be able to eat my favourite pigs in blankets, even if I leave behind the odd bit of turkey."

'My husband's cheating drove me to the brink of suicide'

Jeane Trend-Hill is 50, from London

She says: "I have had some of the worst times of my life over the Christmas period.

Five years ago on Boxing Day I became really ill with a funny tummy which just didn't get better.

I couldn't keep anything down and over the next few weeks my weight dropped to 5st.

I got so weak I ended up in a wheelchair and couldn't do anything for myself.

I was eventually diagnosed with ulcerative colitis - an incurable disease that causes inflammation of the bowels and told I'd need to take 23 pills a day for the rest of my life.

Unable to walk, work or do anything I enjoyed, my mood hit rock bottom.

And then, out of the blue, just before Christmas, my husband of 10 years told me he was leaving me.

I didn't understand it - we'd been happy before I got ill - why had it all changed?

I found out he'd been cheating on me - and as he walked out on me, I looked at the packets of pills in my medicine box and thought: 'What do I have to live for now?'.

I thought how easy it would be to just take them all at once and sink into oblivion.

I sat playing with them for a while and then realised that I would just be making it easy for my husband. He didn't want me anymore and if I died, it was the perfect escape for him.

He would get sympathy, poor wife couldn't cope with her illness and no one would probably ever know he was sleeping around.

I rang the Samaritans, who helped me see there was another way out - that even though things felt awful right now, it wouldn't be this way forever.   

They made me see that nothing that had happened to me was my fault.

YOU'RE NOT ALONE

They helped me make the decision to move in with a friend and I went back to my GP who referred me for therapy.

Over the next few months, I started getting my strength back learned to do things for myself again.

But when Christmas came around again, it brought it all back.

Seeing happy families on TV and in the shops just reminded me that I was alone.

I now try and go away every year to forget what happened - because I hate Christmas with a passion - and it just reminds me of my darkest days."
